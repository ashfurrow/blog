As I mentioned in a [previous post](/blog/lessons-learned-travelling-europe/), I‚Äôve travelled a lot this year, and I‚Äôve just returned home with my wife from our last trip of 2014. It was a whopper: a two-week jaunt starting and ending in Amsterdam and stretching through three other countries. It started a bit over two weeks ago when I left for New York and my wife Ashley left for Nova Scotia to visit her family. We met up a week later in Halifax, drove to my hometown in New Brunswick for a few days‚Äô visit with my family, then back to Halifax for a flight to Reykjavik for a three-day vacation in Iceland. Phew!

I‚Äôm a photographer and will have a post on my [photo blog](https://photos.ashfurrow.com) about Iceland once I get my film back from the development lab. But that takes time, and I feel like I have a broader story to tell, so I‚Äôm going to do a bit of a personal post about my trip.

## New York City

I visited New York City for a week to meet with [Artsy](http://artsy.net). (I was there for meetings; if I were there to work, then I‚Äôd have needed a visa. Since I didn‚Äôt have a visa, I must have been there just for meetings.) I got to experience my [first art auction](/blog/first-art-auction/), meet some friends I knew only online, hang out with my super-cool colleagues, and prepare for moving to NYC in February.

I stayed in Lower Manhattan near Battery Park and walked twenty minutes up to Artsy‚Äôs office at Broadway and Canal. Artsy have just opened up a second floor of the building, which is super-exciting. I got to have coffee with some awesome coworkers and soak in the company culture. After a week of being in the office, I‚Äôm more excited than ever to work at Artsy. The company has an ambitious and laudable goal, an awesome culture, and really smart people. It‚Äôs the kind of company that I hope to start myself someday.

Because I was busy taking advantage of the opportunities to meet so many cool people, I didn‚Äôt have much time to take photos. However, the Artsy office has a great 360¬∫ view of Lower Manhattan, so I took some photos when I could.

While I was excited to see Ashley and my family, I was admittedly sad to leave NYC. Working for Artsy is the first time I‚Äôve ever worked remotely full-time, and I don‚Äôt think that it‚Äôs for me. It‚Äôs so easy to feel disconnected when working remotely, and as much as I feel that I‚Äôm an introvert, I genuinely feel energized when I‚Äôm around my coworkers.

## Atlantic Canada

I flew from LaGuardia to Halifax to meet my wife at the airport. We had rented a vehicle to drive to Woodstock, New Brunswick. We got in pretty late, but managed to get a pizza from our old favourite pizza chain. The pizza place accepted credit cards, but their (actually modern) card machine still used a dial-up modem to process payments.

It‚Äôs weird how things change, but they also stay the same. I left Woodstock in 2006 for university and I‚Äôve only visited it now and then, and not for a few years. Old stores close and new ones open. Houses burn down and new subdivisions are built. But while the same familiar traffic patterns have been repainted an untold number of times since I left in 2006, I still recognize where they are worn away by traffic.

Even though I was glad to see my family and share some meals with them, my dad has to work and my sister is busy in college, so we got to sleep in and enjoy our days however we wanted (after fixing a minor emergency at work early in the week). One day, Ashley and I took a drive to a nearby village, stopping along the Saint John River to take photos. My mom even suggested stopping by the remains of an old train bridge for some photos ‚Äì the same bridge she and my dad had scared me away from playing near when I was a kid (rightly so ‚Äì the thing is a death trap).

Something I‚Äôve wanted to photograph for a few years is the graveyard behind my old house. Most of my shots are on film, so I don‚Äôt have many yet. It was a really interesting experience. I‚Äôve reflected a lot about death and dying, especially with my <a href="/blog/depression-sucks/">depression</a> and my grandmother‚Äôs illness earlier this year (she‚Äôs fine!). Walking through a field specifically set aside in order to bury peoples‚Äô remains, (nominally) ‚Äúforever‚Äù, is weird. It‚Äôs just really really weird. Not creepy ‚Äì just strange. I‚Äôve concluded that cemeteries are more for the living than for the dead.

The next day, we have some goodbyes and begin our nighttime drive back to Halifax, 600km away, to sleep at Ashley‚Äôs dad‚Äôs house for the night. We weren‚Äôt expecting to get to see him or her stepmom, but left Woodstock early due to the weather. It was great to see the two of them again, and the next day, we got to meet with our friend Lauren before flying to Reykjavik.

## Iceland

I flew from Halifax to Toronto then to Reykjavik, while Ashley flew from Halifax to Boston then to Reykjavik. We were worried that the weather would cause one of us to get delayed, but we were lucky enough to both arrive on schedule to make the two hour trip to our secluded cottage on √ûingvallavatn, a lake near [√ûingvellir Park](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/√ûingvellir#Geography). We opted to rent an SUV with a GPS so we a) could go anywhere we wanted to and b) always find our way back.

Sunrise is at about 10:30am this time of year, so by the time we arrived and settled in, the sun was still an hour away from rising. A perfect chance for photos, especially since there was a fresh snowfall from the night before and the landscape looked majestic.

![](shiver.jpg)

We kept taking photos, travelling to a nearby settlement for some supplies and more photos. After getting some breakfast from a hostel there, we explored the waterfront of another lake. They had a geothermic hot tub by the edge of the lake, but we were more interested in the steaming pipes coming up from the boiling sand.

[![Geothermal](geothermal.jpg)](https://500px.com/photo/91489129/geothermal-by-ash-furrow)

We slept that afternoon, since I got very little sleep on the flight over (and Ashley got none at all). We left the cabin after sunset, which is at about 4pm, and took more photos. Using a tripod, we got some really slick long exposures, even in the cold rain.

<Wide>

[![Tracks Home](tracks.jpg)](https://500px.com/photo/91467567/tracks-home-by-ash-furrow)

</Wide>

The next morning, we left before sunrise to get some cool photos from a road we had seen the previous night. All of the snow had melted from the 3¬∫ rain, so it was a completely different landscape.

When we arrived at the spot I wanted to set up, my digital camera wouldn‚Äôt turn on. I hit the power switch and nothing happened. That really sucked. We got some photos with my film cameras (which work without any power ‚Äì take that, analogue photography haters), then headed back to the cabin.

![](dam.jpg)

I made sure the battery was charged (it was) and then threw the camera in a ziplock bag with a half cup of white rice.

![](rice.jpg)

Ten minutes later and the camera worked so we set off. Later, it didn‚Äôt work again, but I put the battery in my pocket for a bit (batteries hate the cold) and the camera back in the bag, and no problem. I don‚Äôt know what the issue was, but if taking photos is an important part of your vacation, having backups is important.

![](selfie.jpg)

When we were planning this vacation, there was one thing I wanted to see more than anything else: in 1973, a US Navy DC‚Äì3 <a href="https://www.google.com/maps/place/63¬∞27'34.3%22N+19¬∞21'52.6%22W/@63.459523,-19.364618,414m/data=!3m1!1e3!4m2!3m1!1s0x0:0x0?hl=en">plane crash-landed</a> on the shores of Southern Iceland. The crew survived and the wreckage remains on the beach today. It was something I wanted to photograph, sure, but more than anything I just wanted to see what a 41-year old plane corpse looked like.
